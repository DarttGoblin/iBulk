Sport

Sport refers to competitive physical activities or games that aim to enhance physical abilities and skills while providing enjoyment to participants and entertainment to spectators. Engaging in sports can lead to improved physical health, whether through casual or organized participation. There are numerous sports, ranging from one-on-one contests to large-scale team competitions. In some sports like racing, participants compete simultaneously or consecutively for a single winner, while others involve matches between two sides vying to outperform each other. Certain sports may end in a "tie" or "draw," with no clear winner, while others employ tie-breaking methods to determine a winner. Tournaments can comprise multiple contests to eventually produce a champion. Many sports leagues determine an annual champion through regular season games followed by playoffs. Sport typically involves physical athleticism or dexterity, and major events like the Olympic Games only recognize sports meeting this criterion. Some non-physical activities, known as mind sports, also seek recognition. The International Olympic Committee acknowledges chess and bridge as sports, and SportAccord recognizes non-physical sports such as bridge, chess, draughts, Go, and xiangqi. Sports are governed by rules or customs that ensure fair competition and consistent determination of winners. Victory can be achieved through physical feats like scoring goals or crossing a finish line first. Judging can also play a role, evaluating elements of a performance based on objective or subjective criteria. Performance records are often maintained, and this information is frequently shared through sports news. Sports provide entertainment to non-participants, drawing large crowds to venues and reaching broader audiences through broadcasting. Sport betting is sometimes tightly regulated and can be integral to the sport itself. As of 2013, the global sports industry is estimated to be worth up to $620 billion. Running is among the most accessible sports, while association football (soccer) is a highly popular spectator sport.


Body Building

Bodybuilding involves the application of progressive resistance exercises to sculpt and enhance muscles, resulting in muscle hypertrophy for aesthetic purposes. Unlike activities like powerlifting that emphasize strength, bodybuilding focuses on achieving a pleasing physical appearance. Those who partake in this pursuit are known as bodybuilders. In the realm of professional bodybuilding, competitors participate in lineups and execute prescribed poses, followed by individual posing routines, all evaluated by a panel of judges. Judging criteria encompass symmetry, muscularity, size, conditioning, posing ability, and stage presentation. The preparation for these competitions entails shedding unnecessary body fat, often achieved through a combination of extracellular dehydration and carbo-loading in the final stages to maximize muscle definition and vascularity. Additional efforts include tanning and shaving to highlight skin contrast under stage lighting. An example of a bodybuilding exercise is the incline dumbbell press. Achieving the desired results in bodybuilding demands substantial effort and time. In the early stages, a bodybuilder might gain around 8–20 pounds (4–9 kg) of muscle annually with seven hours of weekly training. However, this rate of gain tends to slow after the initial two years, dropping to approximately 5–15 pounds (2–7 kg). Beyond five years, progress can further decline to 3–10 pounds (1–5 kg). While some individuals turn to anabolic steroids and other performance-enhancing substances to accelerate muscle growth and injury recovery, their usage is often prohibited in competitions due to health risks and concerns about fair play. Notably, the primary bodybuilding federation, the National Physique Committee, does not mandate steroid testing despite occasional calls for such measures.


History

The practice of lifting stones dates back to ancient civilizations such as Egypt, Greece, and Tamilakam. The tradition of stone-lifting has historical roots in these regions. In the Western context, weightlifting started to develop in Europe between 1880 and 1953. During this time, strongmen showcased impressive feats of strength to the public and engaged in friendly challenges among themselves. Interestingly, their focus was not on achieving a sculpted physique; they often had larger bellies and less defined limbs compared to modern bodybuilders. Eugen Sandow, a German individual often referred to as the "Father of Modern Bodybuilding," played a significant role in the evolution of bodybuilding. He emerged in the late 19th century and gained prominence in England. Sandow introduced the concept of "muscle display performances," allowing audiences to appreciate his well-developed physique. While these displays were a source of excitement, they were initially presented as part of strength demonstrations and wrestling matches. Sandow collaborated with Florenz Ziegfeld, his manager, to create a stage show that showcased his physique for carnival-goers. The 1936 musical film "The Great Ziegfeld," which won an Oscar, portrays the beginnings of modern bodybuilding and highlights how Sandow's practice of displaying his physique gained popularity, particularly in carnival settings. Sandow's success extended beyond his performances. He capitalized on his fame by establishing several businesses and was among the first individuals to market products branded with his name. He is credited with inventing and selling exercise equipment accessible to the masses, including machined dumbbells, spring pulleys, and tension bands. Sandow even found commercial success in selling images of himself, which were widely distributed in various forms such as "cabinet cards" and prints. His contributions and innovations left a lasting impact on the development of modern bodybuilding and fitness culture.


Competitions

Eugen Sandow, a pivotal figure in modern bodybuilding, organized the inaugural bodybuilding contest known as the "Great Competition" on September 14, 1901. This event took place at London's Royal Albert Hall. The judging panel included Sandow himself, along with Sir Charles Lawes and Sir Arthur Conan Doyle. The competition garnered immense popularity, leading to a surplus of enthusiastic attendees and turning away many due to limited space. The winner, William L. Murray of Nottingham, received a gold statue of Sandow sculpted by Frederick Pomeroy. Second place, awarded to D. Cooper, received a silver Sandow trophy, while the now-famous bronze Sandow trophy was bestowed upon third-place winner A.C. Smythe. This bronze trophy later reappeared in the bodybuilding scene in 1950, when it was awarded to Steve Reeves, the victor of the inaugural NABBA Mr. Universe contest. The trophy resurfaced again in 1977 when Frank Zane, the winner of the IFBB Mr. Olympia contest, was presented with a replica of the bronze trophy. Subsequently, winners of the Mr. Olympia competition have consistently received replicas of this iconic Sandow trophy. In the United States, the first significant bodybuilding competition occurred from December 28, 1903, to January 2, 1904, at Madison Square Garden in New York City. This event, organized by Bernarr Macfadden, a pioneer in the field of physical culture and publisher of bodybuilding magazines, crowned Al Treloar as "The Most Perfectly Developed Man in the World." Treloar won a substantial cash prize of $1,000, an impressive sum for that era. Thomas Edison also contributed to bodybuilding's history by filming Treloar's posing routine, which marked one of the earliest instances of capturing a bodybuilder on film. Throughout the early 20th century, figures like Macfadden and Charles Atlas played vital roles in promoting bodybuilding internationally. Competitive bodybuilding is characterized by participants aiming to present a visually appealing physique on stage. Prejudging involves a series of mandatory poses, showcasing different muscle groups, such as the lat spread, double biceps, chest, and triceps poses. Competitors also perform personalized choreographed routines to highlight their physique. A posedown, where participants display their poses one final time, often concludes a posing round. In contrast to other strength-focused competitions like strongman or powerlifting, where physical strength takes precedence, and Olympic weightlifting, which emphasizes both strength and technique, bodybuilding competitions prioritize factors like muscular condition, size, and symmetry. Various organizations may emphasize specific aspects of competition and offer distinct categories for competitors to engage in.


Heroes

In addition to Eugen Sandow, there were several other influential bodybuilders who made significant contributions to the field before 1930. These individuals played crucial roles in shaping the early history of bodybuilding: Earle Liederman: Known for writing some of the earliest books on bodybuilding and fitness. Zishe Breitbart: Noted for his exceptional strength and feats of strength displays. Georg Hackenschmidt: A wrestler and strongman who contributed to the development of bodybuilding. Emy Nkemena: An early bodybuilder who made an impact in the field. George F. Jowett: A prominent figure in physical culture and bodybuilding. Finn Hateral: A pioneer in the art of posing, which is a crucial aspect of competitive bodybuilding. Frank Saldo and Monte Saldo: Notable figures in the early bodybuilding scene. William Bankier: Contributed to the early growth of bodybuilding. Launceston Elliot: An important figure in the history of bodybuilding and weightlifting. Sig Klein: A well-known bodybuilder and strongman of his time. Sgt. Alfred Moss: Made contributions to the field despite his military service. Joe Nordquist: Played a role in the early bodybuilding movement. Lionel Strongfort ("Strongfortism"): Known for his strength and physical culture teachings. Gustav Frištenský: An influential figure in the early bodybuilding community. Ralph Parcaut: A champion wrestler who also authored a book on "physical culture." Alan P. Mead: Overcame challenges, including losing a leg in World War I, to become a respected muscle champion. It's interesting to note that even actor Francis X. Bushman, who was a follower of Eugen Sandow, started his career as a bodybuilder and model for sculptors before becoming a famous silent movie star. These early pioneers collectively laid the foundation for the growth and development of modern bodybuilding.


Modern Body Building

In 2003, Joe Weider made the decision to sell Weider Publications to American Media, Inc. (AMI). After the passing of Ben Weider in October 2008, Rafael Santonja assumed the position of president of the International Federation of Bodybuilding and Fitness (IFBB). In 2004, contest promoter Wayne DeMilia broke away from the IFBB, and the promotion of the Mr. Olympia contest was taken over by AMI. Eventually, in 2017, AMI gained full ownership of the Mr. Olympia contest. During the early 21st century, trends in consumption and leisure activities resembling those in the United States began to spread more widely across Europe, particularly in Eastern Europe after the collapse of the Soviet Union. This shift led to the emergence of new communities of bodybuilders from the former Eastern Bloc nations. These changes in societal patterns contributed to the growth and diversification of bodybuilding in these regions. This transformation showcases the global influence and evolution of bodybuilding, with changes in ownership, organizational leadership, and the expansion of the sport's reach to new populations around the world.


Advices

Bodybuilders, pretty impressive aren’t they? They’re strong, in control and look great. So, it’s no surprise that more men than ever are interested in giving bodybuilding a go themselves. Starting bodybuilding for the first time can be daunting, especially as there seems to be a lot to consider and a lot to learn. However, with these simple tips, you’ll be ready to go in no time.

1. Master Proper Form First

Before you do anything in bodybuilding, it’s vital that you master proper form. Otherwise, you’ll pick up bad habits that are hard to break. There’s a lot of help available online, so ensure you do your research and perfect it.

2. Take a Day Off Between Workouts

When you start off bodybuilding it can be tempting to work at it every day, but this isn’t always best. Make sure you give your body a day off between workouts to recover, repair and relax.

3. Make Sure You Stretch

As with any kind of workout, it’s important to stretch before bodybuilding. Stretching decreases the chances of damage and soreness. Plus, it increases the amount of motion possible and you’ll be able to put more into your different lifts.

4. Have a Goal

It can be easy to start bodybuilding and expect major results instantly, but that isn’t going to happen. In order to keep on track and to keep yourself motivated, have a realistic goal and a deadline. Start off small and increase your goals as you improve.

5. Eat Before and After a Workout

What you eat before and after a workout with have a significant impact on how well that workout goes. As a beginner you won’t be focusing on the specifics of your food intake, but trying to stock up on protein and carbohydrates before and after is helpful.

6. Don’t Forget Cardio

A lot of beginners focus all of their energy on bodybuilding lifts and forget about cardio. For best results, save cardio until after you have completed your workout and focus the majority of your energy on lifting.

7. Aim for Eight to Twelve Reps

Beginners are advised to aim for between eight and twelve reps of a lift when first starting out. This is because you are likely to begin with lifting slightly lighter weights and therefore more reps are possible. As you begin to lift heavier weights, lower the reps.

8. Stay Focused and In Control

It’s easy to get carried away when you first begin, especially when you get into the swing of a workout and a lift becomes easier. However, rather than rushing through, focus on staying in control and perfecting the task.

9. Focus on Compound Movements

A lot of bodybuilding techniques are complex, so start by focusing on the easier compound movements; squats, shoulder presses, deadlifts and lunges all work well. These require you to use multiple muscle groups at the same time and therefore more muscles are worked overall, without the need to add on individual movements.

10. Learn from Your Mistakes

Everyone makes mistakes, that’s a part of life. Rather than letting them put you off, learn from them. If you find yourself struggling with a workout routine, change it up! Keep alternating and editing until you find a bodybuilding program that works for you.
